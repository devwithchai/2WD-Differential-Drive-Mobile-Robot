# Abstract

The foundation of every reliable mobile robot is a thoroughly validated hardware platform. Before implementing wireless communication, sensing, localization, or autonomous navigation, it is essential to ensure that the drive system operates correctly under all expected motion conditions.

This report documents the first development stage of the **2WD Differential Drive Mobile Robot**, focusing on hardware bring-up and validation of the drivetrain. The primary objective was to establish reliable control of two DC geared motors using an Arduino UNO and an L298N dual H-bridge motor driver.

A series of incremental experiments were conducted, beginning with individual motor verification and progressing toward complete differential drive motion. The robot was tested for forward motion, backward motion, left rotation, right rotation, and stop functionality. Hardware wiring, motor direction logic, and software implementation were systematically validated.

Successful completion of this level established a stable hardware baseline for subsequent integration of Bluetooth communication, distributed embedded control, ROS 2, simulation, odometry, and autonomous navigation.

---

# 1. Introduction

Mobile robots are widely used in industrial automation, logistics, service robotics, warehouse transportation, agricultural robotics, and autonomous vehicles. Among the various mobile robot configurations, the **differential drive robot** is one of the most widely adopted because of its simple mechanical design, ease of control, and compatibility with robotic navigation algorithms.

A differential drive robot consists of two independently driven wheels positioned on either side of the chassis, typically supported by a passive caster wheel. By independently controlling the direction and speed of each wheel, the robot can move forward, backward, rotate about its center, or follow curved trajectories.

Although the mechanical architecture appears simple, the overall reliability of the robot depends heavily on the correct integration of electrical hardware and embedded software. Incorrect wiring, reversed motor polarity, unstable power connections, or software errors can prevent higher-level robotic algorithms from functioning correctly.

For this reason, Level 1 of this project focuses exclusively on validating the complete drive subsystem before introducing communication modules or advanced robotics software.

# 3. Background Theory

## 3.1 Differential Drive Mobile Robot

A differential drive robot is a non-holonomic mobile robot whose motion is generated by independently controlling the left and right wheels.

The robot's motion depends on the relative motion of the two drive wheels:

| Left Wheel | Right Wheel | Robot Motion |
|------------|-------------|--------------|
| Forward | Forward | Forward |
| Backward | Backward | Backward |
| Backward | Forward | Rotate Left |
| Forward | Backward | Rotate Right |
| Stop | Stop | Stationary |

Unlike omnidirectional robots, a differential drive robot cannot move sideways directly. Instead, it changes its orientation through differential wheel motion.

Because of their simplicity and robustness, differential drive robots are widely used in educational robotics, warehouse AGVs, research platforms, and service robots.

---

## 3.2 L298N Motor Driver

The Arduino UNO is capable of producing only low-current digital control signals. These signals cannot directly power DC motors because motors require significantly higher current than a microcontroller output pin can safely provide.

To overcome this limitation, an **L298N dual H-bridge motor driver** is used.

The L298N performs two important functions:

- Supplies sufficient current to the motors.
- Reverses motor polarity electronically, allowing bidirectional rotation.

Each motor is controlled using two logic inputs.

For Motor A:

- IN1
- IN2

For Motor B:

- IN3
- IN4

By changing the logic state of these inputs, the motor driver determines the direction of motor rotation.

---

## 3.3 H-Bridge Principle

An H-Bridge is an electronic switching circuit that allows current to flow through a DC motor in either direction.

Reversing current direction reverses motor rotation.

This principle forms the basis of differential drive motion and is one of the most fundamental concepts in embedded motor control.

# 5. System Architecture

The Level 1 architecture was intentionally kept simple to validate the motor control subsystem independently before introducing communication modules or higher-level software.

The system consists of four major components:

1. Arduino UNO
2. L298N Dual H-Bridge Motor Driver
3. Differential Drive Motors
4. Battery Power Supply

The Arduino generates digital control signals that determine the direction of motor rotation. These logic signals are processed by the L298N motor driver, which supplies sufficient current to the motors using power from the battery pack.

System Architecture:

```
        Arduino UNO
             │
             │ Digital Outputs
             ▼
     L298N Motor Driver
             │
     ┌───────┴────────┐
     ▼                ▼
 Left Motor      Right Motor
```

This modular architecture allows the communication subsystem to be integrated in later stages without modifying the motor control logic.

# 6. Circuit Connections

The following connections were implemented between the Arduino UNO and the L298N motor driver.

## Arduino to L298N

| Arduino UNO | L298N |
|-------------|--------|
| D2 | IN1 |
| D3 | IN2 |
| D4 | IN3 |
| D5 | IN4 |
| GND | GND |
| 5V | 5V |

Motor Driver Configuration:

- ENA → Enabled using jumper
- ENB → Enabled using jumper

Since PWM speed control was not implemented in Level 1, both enable pins remained permanently HIGH through jumper connections.

The motors were connected to:

- OUT1 & OUT2 → Left Motor
- OUT3 & OUT4 → Right Motor

Power was supplied using two 3.7V Li-ion cells connected to the motor driver.

# 7. Motion Logic

Differential drive motion is achieved by controlling the direction of rotation of the left and right wheels.

The implemented motion logic is shown below.

| Motion | IN1 | IN2 | IN3 | IN4 |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| Forward | LOW | HIGH | HIGH | LOW |
| Backward | HIGH | LOW | LOW | HIGH |
| Left Rotation | HIGH | LOW | HIGH | LOW |
| Right Rotation | LOW | HIGH | LOW | HIGH |
| Stop | LOW | LOW | LOW | LOW |

The software implementation was divided into reusable motion functions:

- forward()
- backward()
- left()
- right()
- stopRobot()

This modular design simplifies maintenance and enables future communication modules to invoke robot motion using simple function calls.

---

# 8. Arduino Software Design

The software architecture follows a modular function-based design.

```
setup()

↓

Initialize Output Pins

↓

loop()

↓

Call Motion Function

↓

Delay

↓

Call Stop Function

↓

Repeat
```

Each motion primitive was implemented as an independent function responsible only for setting the required motor driver logic.

Example:

```
forward()

↓

Motor A → Forward

↓

Motor B → Forward
```

This approach improves code readability and promotes software reuse in subsequent development stages.

# 11. Challenges and Debugging

During hardware bring-up, several practical issues were encountered.

## Issue 1 – Motor Not Rotating

Observation:

One motor failed to rotate during initial testing.

Root Cause:

Loose connection between the motor terminals and the L298N output.

Resolution:

The wiring was inspected and reconnected securely.

Result:

Both motors operated correctly.

---

## Issue 2 – Incorrect Rotation Direction

Observation:

Initial wheel rotation did not correspond to the intended robot motion.

Root Cause:

Motor polarity required adjustment.

Resolution:

Motor logic was verified and updated until the desired differential drive behaviour was achieved.

---

## Issue 3 – Incremental Testing

Rather than testing all motion commands simultaneously, individual motor verification was performed first.

This significantly simplified debugging and reduced troubleshooting time.

# 12. Engineering Decisions

Several design decisions were intentionally made during Level 1.

| Decision | Justification |
|----------|---------------|
| Arduino UNO selected as motor controller | Simple, reliable, and well supported for hardware validation |
| L298N selected as motor driver | Widely used educational dual H-bridge driver suitable for differential drive robots |
| Modular motion functions | Simplifies future software integration |
| ESP32 not introduced | Hardware validation should precede communication integration |
| Fixed speed operation | Focus placed on directional control before introducing PWM |

These decisions reduced project complexity while establishing a reliable hardware foundation.

---

# 13. Lessons Learned

The completion of Level 1 provided several important engineering insights.

- Hardware validation should always precede software integration.
- Small wiring errors can produce behaviour that appears to be software related.
- Modular software architecture greatly simplifies future expansion.
- Incremental subsystem testing is significantly more effective than testing the complete system immediately.
- Reliable debugging depends on changing only one variable at a time.

These lessons formed the engineering methodology followed throughout the remainder of the project.
